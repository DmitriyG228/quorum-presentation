## TL;DR
Franklin Noll from the Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City discusses how US government perceptions of stablecoins have evolved through three distinct narratives: from "shadow money" (Libra era), to "wildcat money," and finally to "future money." The presentation examines Congressional concerns, regulatory approaches, and the evolving policy landscape around cryptocurrency stablecoins.

### Three Narratives of Stablecoins

#### Shadow Money Narrative (2019)
The first narrative emerged with Meta's Libra proposal in June 2019. The original Libra concept featured:
- Backing by a basket of different currencies
- A consortium of 40 companies including Visa and PayPal
- Positioned as a global payment system

Congressional reaction was highly negative, particularly from Democratic leadership:
- **Key Decision:** Maxine Waters (House) and Sherrod Brown (Senate) led opposition
- Primary concern: Establishment of a "shadow banking" system that could rival the US dollar
- Perception: Facebook was attempting to create an unregulated financial system based in Switzerland

#### Wildcat Money Narrative
The narrative evolved as policy makers began comparing stablecoins to historical "wildcat banks" — unregulated or loosely regulated banking systems that issued their own currency with varying credibility.

#### Future Money Narrative (2021-2024)
The most recent narrative frames stablecoins as legitimate infrastructure for the future of money, reflecting shifting regulatory attitudes and technological maturation. This narrative emerged in late 2021 with the President's Working Group report coining the term "payment stable coins" — defined as stablecoins with stable value pegged to fiat currency, designed for widespread use as a retail payment medium.

Key milestones:
- November 2021: President's Working Group introduces "payment stable coins" terminology
- 2022: Congress adopts the term in proposed legislation
- S.534 (Sen. Toomey & Sen. Lumis/Gillibrand): Defines payment stable coins with Federal Reserve oversight provisions
- HR 4766 (Rep. McHenry): "Clarity for Payment Stable Coins Act" — not yet passed to House floor vote

**Key Quote from [[Nelly Liang]] (Biden Treasury Under Secretary):** "Stable coins are a cornerstone of a new payment system"

### Comparative Analysis: 2019 vs. 2024
- **2019 Perception:** Threat to global economy and US dollar; shadow banking system; Libra to be banned
- **2024 Perception:** Vital to US competitiveness; will ensure dollar dominance; foundation of future payment infrastructure

### Causation: Why Narratives Changed
According to Franklin Noll's analysis:
1. **Shadow Money → Wildcat Money:** Meta/Facebook's unpopularity and abandonment of Libra reduced visceral opposition; [[Brian Brooks]] at OCC began issuing pro-stablecoin interpretive letters (Sept 2020, Jan 2021)
2. **Wildcat Money → Future Money:** Driven by 2021 crypto bull market, NFT hype, Metaverse discussions, and major exchange public offerings (Coinbase IPO on NASDAQ); USD-pegged stablecoin market cap surged from ~$20B to ~$100B+; President's Working Group shift toward "payment stable coins" framing enabled bipartisan support

## Key Decisions
- The presentation frames stablecoin policy evolution as driven by shifting narratives rather than purely technical or economic factors
- US government perspective emphasizes the importance of regulatory clarity and consumer protection
- Debate over regulatory regime: Republicans prefer state control, Democrats prefer Federal Reserve oversight

## Q&A Session

### CBDCs and Stablecoin Narratives
**Q (Alonso):** How do the changes in narratives relate to the surge of CBDCs?
**A (Franklin Noll):** The timing is interesting—in November 2021, when the payment stable coins concept emerged, a White House report was pushing CBDCs strongly. He speculates that Republican opposition to government-issued CBDCs (especially [[Tom Emmer]]) may have driven support for privately-issued stablecoins as an alternative that accomplishes similar goals. This could be a partisan positioning issue, though the connection requires deeper investigation into Congressional staffers' thinking.

### Real-World Use Cases
**Q (Alexander Gloy):** What problem are stablecoins trying to solve? Why can't it be solved within existing payment rails and institutions?
**A (Franklin Noll):**
- Current real-world use cases are limited to remittances (Stellar's system, USDC via wallet-to-wallet transfers avoiding Western Union fees)
- Retail use case at Starbucks doesn't exist yet and may not be necessary (users can already pay with phones)
- Potential future use case: local private currencies issued by cities (e.g., Pittsburgh) with revenue reinvestment in local infrastructure
- A company-issued stablecoin with reward points (similar to credit card loyalty programs) could create demand

**Response (Counterargument):** 3% transaction fees embedded in current payment systems are passed to consumers through higher merchant prices. CBDCs would reduce this to nearly zero. With stablecoins, merchants bear costs but still charge the 3% pass-through.

### Stablecoin Stability and Par Risk
**Discussion on Par Maintenance:** Referenced [[Perry Merling]]'s work on "par" comparing stablecoins to Eurodollars. Key insight: maintaining par (1:1 peg to dollar) becomes difficult at boundaries (geographic, blockchain-based). Commercial bank money achieves frictionless parity with cash through sophisticated mechanisms that stablecoins lack.

**Stability Under Stress:** Stablecoins have broken par multiple times (like money market funds in 2008). Overcollateralized crypto stablecoins (e.g., DAI) face rapid liquidation risk due to crypto volatility.

### Federal Reserve and Seigniorage
**Q:** Is the Fed worried about Tether/stablecoins capturing seigniorage ("eating the Fed's lunch")?
**A (Franklin Noll):** Tether volume is negligible compared to private bank deposits and seigniorage. However, smaller countries may rely heavily on stablecoin reserves. Federal Reserve focuses on monetary policy, not profit/loss from seigniorage.

### Cross-Border Transactions
**Q (Money Play):** Will upcoming US regulations permit cross-border transactions with payment stablecoins?
**A (Franklin Noll):** Congressional bills don't address cross-border use—Congress is focused on domestic stability and AML/KYC compliance. Cross-border adoption of USD-pegged stablecoins would reinforce dollar supremacy globally, similar to how Eurodollars function.

**Related Context ([[Perry Merling]]'s "Fit for Purpose" paper from Federal Reserve, 2022):** Discusses interoperability mechanisms and the "money view" combining monetary and financial perspectives—culture and strategy are key factors in adoption, alongside technology.

### Asset-Backed vs. Currency-Backed Stablecoins
**Q:** Can stablecoins be backed by environmental health or carbon removals rather than dollars?
**A:** Not according to emerging US legislation (Congress defines payment stablecoins as fiat-pegged). Theoretically possible with algorithmic or alternative peg mechanisms, but not the regulatory focus. DAI (Ethereum stablecoin) maintains dollar peg via overcollateralization with crypto, but this is fragile.

### Closing Remarks
Franklin Noll emphasized that stablecoin stability challenges (runs, par breaks, speed of redemption vs. reserve duration mismatches) are well-known in traditional finance and crypto, and require deep systemic thinking similar to Merling's money view framework.
